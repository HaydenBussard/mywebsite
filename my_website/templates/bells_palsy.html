Bell's Palsy Resources

The day before my Bell's Palsy onset, I experienced persistent, low-pitched ear ringing.

Unlike some who experience gradual facial paralysis, mine occurred suddenly overnight. I woke up unable to blink my right eye or move that side of my face.

Despite the shock, being aware of Bell's Palsy helped me stay calm. I can't imagine how frightening this would be for those unfamiliar with the condition.

My doctor advised waiting it out, assuring me that Bell's Palsy is typically temporary. Improvements usually start in 2-3 weeks, with full recovery in 2-6 months.

However, 14 months later, I still have Bell's Palsy. I saw no improvement for the first 4 months, which indicated a longer recovery ahead.

While not impossible to live with, Bell's Palsy can be annoying and embarrassing. Here are two crucial tips for those newly diagnosed:

  1. Socialize with loved ones. Their acceptance will help you maintain confidence in public.
  2. Protect your affected eye. I've linked helpful products below.

I used goggles from day one for showering to keep soap and water out of my eye.

Over time, my improvements reached a point where most people don't notice I have Bell's Palsy. It's only noticeable in certain circumstances, like squinting in sunlight or laughing.

For me, after my muscles went so long without receiving signals, I developed a muscle memory issue. My eye on the affected side closes on its own when I'm laughing hard, chewing food, or yawning. However, I know that physical therapy is an option to retrain those muscles.
